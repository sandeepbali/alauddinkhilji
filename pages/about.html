Our Purpose

The Mission

This website is part of the Bharat Files Initiative, dedicated to uncovering and documenting the real, unvarnished history of India using primary historical sources. The initiative maintains 15+ dedicated archives covering key historical figures whose true impact on Indian civilization has been systematically obscured, sanitized, or omitted from mainstream education.

The Alauddin Khilji archive is one of the most comprehensive in the network. It brings together primary Persian historical texts, modern academic scholarship, and archaeological evidence to construct a complete, verifiable account of Khilji's twenty-year reign and its devastating impact on Indian civilization. Every claim is footnoted. Every source is cited. Every reader is invited to verify independently.

Our commitment: We present what the historical record says, as recorded by the historians of the period themselves. We do not fabricate, exaggerate, or sensationalize. The primary sources speak for themselves — and they are devastating enough without embellishment.

The Context

Why This Matters

Indian education has systematically sanitized accounts of medieval-era invasions and their impact on the subcontinent's people, culture, and civilization. For decades, textbooks presented the Delhi Sultanate primarily as a period of administrative innovation and cultural synthesis, while omitting or minimizing the documented temple destructions, mass killings, economic subjugation, and religious persecution that primary sources describe in extensive detail.

This creates a dangerous disconnect between lived reality and official narrative. Millions of Indians live among the ruins of a civilization that was systematically dismantled — broken temple pillars repurposed into mosque columns, sacred sites converted or destroyed, cultural traditions disrupted — yet the education system that shaped their understanding of history offered no explanation for what they could see with their own eyes.

This archive aims to bridge that gap with source-backed documentation. Not with rhetoric or emotion, but with the words of the court historians who served these rulers, the archaeological evidence preserved in the ground and in standing structures, and the analysis of leading modern scholars who have studied the primary sources.

A Note on Our Approach

This archive does not seek to inflame hatred or promote division. It seeks to present documented historical facts that have been suppressed or obscured. Understanding what happened — accurately and completely — is a prerequisite for genuine reconciliation, informed citizenship, and the protection of the cultural heritage that survived. Ignorance is not the foundation of harmony; truth is.

How We Work

Our Methodology

Every claim on this website follows a rigorous three-step verification process grounded in established historical methodology.

1

Primary Source Foundation

Every historical claim begins with primary sources — texts written during or shortly after the events they describe. For Alauddin Khilji's reign, these include Ziauddin Barani's Tarikh-i-Firoz Shahi, Amir Khusrau's Khazain-ul-Futuh, and Isami's Futuh-us-Salatin. These Persian chronicles were written by Muslim court historians sympathetic to the Sultanate, making their accounts of atrocities particularly reliable — the violence they record was presented as achievement, not accusation.

2

Modern Scholarly Corroboration

Primary source claims are corroborated through modern academic scholarship. We draw on the work of leading historians including K.S. Lal, R.C. Majumdar, Richard Eaton, Irfan Habib, and others whose peer-reviewed work has analyzed, contextualized, and interpreted the primary sources through modern historical methodology.

3

Archaeological & Physical Evidence

Where possible, written accounts are supported by archaeological evidence from the Archaeological Survey of India (ASI) and other professional archaeological bodies. Physical evidence at sites such as Chittor Fort, the Qutb Complex, and the Somnath temple corroborates the written record with tangible, inspectable proof.
